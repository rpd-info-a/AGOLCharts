Violent Crime Comparison

This chart shows most cities in our sample clustering between 900 and 1,300 violent crimes per 100,000 persons per year. There are several outliers, with Pontiac, Saginaw and St. Louis having much higher average violent crime rates. Rochester’s average rate for this time period is 996 or 6th lowest out of the 21 total cities. Rochester’s rate has remained fairly stable across the time period, never peaking above 1,260 and remaining consistently below the yearly combined cities’ average. Buffalo, the most similar comparison city, had an average rate of 1,372 across this time period. Rochester’s 2017 rate was 897, eighth lowest out of the twenty-one cities. In 2017, Rochester was sixth for rape, fifteenth for robbery, and sixth for aggravated assault (which, along with murder/manslaughter, are the component crimes of violent crime).

Property Crime Comparison

This chart shows that the overall property crime rate in all cities is decreasing. Every single city had lower property crime rates in 2017 than 1985. In many cases, rates are half those in the 1980s. Rochester’s declines have mirrored the broader national declines, falling from over 10,000 per year to less than 4,000 in 2017. Over the entire time period, Rochester’s property crime rate is the 6th highest out of the 21 cities; however property crime has been declining faster in Rochester than in comparable cities. Limiting the look to the most recent ten-year period (2007–2016), Rochester ranks 9th lowest out of 21. Rochester’s 2017 rate was 3,803, continuing the trend of decreasing property crime rates, positioning Rochester as the ninth lowest out of the twenty-one cities.

Homicide Comparison

St Louis and Richmond are the clear outliers in this group of cities. 2003 appears to be an abnormally low year for St. Louis, and a small spike for Rochester, resulting in the only year that Rochester had a higher rate than St. Louis. Starting in 2005, Richmond’s rate plummets, and rejoins the cluster of cities that have rates between 10 and 20 homicides per 100,000 persons per year. In contrast to Richmond, Akron and Wilmington appear to be trending upwards. The rate of Buffalo, the most similar comparison city, stays close to the rate of Rochester throughout the time period. Rochester’s average homicide rate across the time period was 19.0, or 7th highest out of the 21 cities. Buffalo’s average is 17.2, and St Louis’s is 44.2. Schenectady, Lansing, and Grand Rapids all had averages under 9. Rochester’s 2017 rate was 12.9, which was the lowest since 1999 and third lowest since 1985. Rochester ranked was 7th lowest in 2017 out of the 21 cities.

Robbery Comparison

This chart shows the majority of cities in the past three years have a rate between 200 and 400 robberies per year. Rochester has been in the middle of that range since 2010, with the exception of a spike in the 2013 rate to 436. Cleveland, St. Louis, Wilmington, and Milwaukee have been the cities with the highest rates the past three years, well above 500. Lansing had the lowest rate of these cities every year between 2000 and 2014 with rates in the low 200’s until 2015 when several cities experienced severe drops in reported robbery, with Schenectady the lowest at 155. Rochester also experienced a drop in 2015, leading to Rochester’s lowest robbery rate in this time period, 305. Rochester experienced a slight rise in 2017 in robberies, but the overall rate remains consistent with 2016 levels at 339. This rate is the seventh highest out of the twenty-one cities.

Aggravated Assault

Rochester’s aggravated assault rate stays consistently below the average rate of these 21 cities. Across the time period, Rochester has the 3rd lowest aggravated assault rate, at 436 per year. Two small cities in Michigan, Saginaw (1,761) and Pontiac (1,464) have the highest rate for this time period, followed by St Louis at 1,432. Rochester’s 2017 numbers rank it at sixth lowest out of twenty-one, at 463. Richmond has the lowest rate for 2017 with 293, while St. Louis has the highest with 1,297.

Burglary Comparison

This chart shows collective burglary rates declining from the 1980s and 1990s to the current time, with a slight uptick in 2010 and 2011. Rochester’s rate mirrors this trend. While Rochester’s burglary rate in the 1980s and early 1990s was in the upper half of these cities (7th highest in 1985 and 1986, 4th in 1989, 2nd in 1992, 3rd in 1993), Rochester’s rate declined faster than most comparison cities throughout the 1990s. In 2001 and 2002, Rochester ranked 3rd and 2nd lowest respectively. A slight rise in the late 2000s resulted in a recent peak in 2010, but Rochester has observed consecutive declines every year since then. Rochester’s 2017 burglary numbers were the lowest in 30 years, continuing the downward trend and resulting in the 4th lowest rate of the 21 cities.

Larceny Comparison

Larceny, like burglary, spiked in the early 1990s and has declined steadily since (without burglary’s spike in the late 2000s). Unlike burglary, Rochester has consistently had higher than average larceny rates, compared with the other twenty cities. Rochester has been experiencing declines in larceny rates similar to the other cities, but starting from a higher baseline. Those declines resulted in Rochester ending 2016 in the middle of this group of cities, with the eleventh highest rate out of the twenty-one cities, at a rate of 2,755 larcenies per year. Rochester’s 2017 rate increased slightly, to 2,864, resulting in Rochester dropping to fourteenth lowest of the cities. Despite the increase, 2017 resulted in the second lowest rate and second lowest raw number of larcenies in Rochester’s previous 30 years.
